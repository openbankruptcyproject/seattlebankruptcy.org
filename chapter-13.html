Chapter 13 Bankruptcy in Seattle

Chapter 13 (sometimes called "wage-earner bankruptcy" or a "reorganization plan") gives a debtor with regular income a path to keep property and repay creditors over 3-5 years. Unlike Chapter 7, Chapter 13 does not require liquidating non-exempt assets. Filers commit a portion of their disposable monthly income to a court-approved repayment plan.

Why people choose Chapter 13

  • Save a home from foreclosure by curing missed mortgage payments through the plan.
  • Cram down secured debt on certain non-residential collateral (older cars, investment property).
  • Income too high for Chapter 7 per the means test.
  • Discharge through completion -- if you finish the plan, qualifying debts are discharged.

What plan completion looks like in the W.D. Wash.

Based on FJC data covering 513,422 cases in the W.D. Wash., the Chapter 13 dismissal rate is 9.3%. Dismissal means the case ended without a discharge -- usually due to missed plan payments or a failed amendment. The remaining filers either completed their plan, converted to Chapter 7, or have cases still pending.

Plan duration

Plans run 3 years (36 months) for below-median-income filers, or 5 years (60 months) for above-median-income. The trustee distributes plan payments to creditors per the priority hierarchy: secured creditors first, then priority (taxes, support), then general unsecured.

Eligibility limits

Chapter 13 has debt ceilings (adjusted periodically): generally about $2.75M of combined secured and unsecured debt as of 2026. Above that ceiling, individual filers may need Chapter 11 instead.

Washington exemptions still apply

Homestead -- Washington homestead exemption protects up to the county median home price (varies by county, $172,900-$729,600). Vehicle -- Washington exempts up to $3,250 in motor vehicle equity. Exemptions matter in Chapter 13 because they set the floor on what you must pay unsecured creditors (the "best interests" test).
