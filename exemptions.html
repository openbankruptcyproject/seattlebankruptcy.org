Washington Bankruptcy Exemptions

Bankruptcy exemptions are the legal rules that protect property from being taken by the trustee or creditors. Each state sets its own exemption schedule, and federal exemptions exist as an alternative for filers in opt-in states.

Homestead

Washington homestead exemption protects up to the county median home price (varies by county, $172,900-$729,600).

Motor Vehicle

Washington exempts up to $3,250 in motor vehicle equity.

Personal property

Washington also exempts (in varying amounts depending on the statute): clothing, household furnishings, books, tools of the trade, jewelry up to a cap, family heirlooms, and unmatured life insurance. Verify exact dollar amounts with current Washington statutes -- bankruptcy exemption schedules update for inflation periodically.

Retirement accounts

ERISA-qualified retirement plans (401(k), 403(b), most pensions) are fully exempt. IRAs and Roth IRAs are exempt up to a federal cap (currently $1,512,350 across all accounts, adjusted every three years). Inherited IRAs may not be exempt -- see Clark v. Rameker (2014).

Federal exemptions alternative (11 U.S.C. § 522(d))

Some states (opt-in) allow filers to choose either the state schedule or the federal schedule. Other states (opt-out) restrict you to state exemptions only. The federal homestead exemption is $27,900 (single) / $55,800 (joint), and the federal vehicle exemption is $4,450. See 11 U.S.C. § 522(d) for the full federal schedule.

Wildcard exemption

Many state schedules include a "wildcard" exemption that can be applied to any property. Wildcards are useful when you have non-protected cash or unusual assets that don't fit the categorized exemptions.

Domicile rule

To use a state's exemptions, you must have lived there for the prior 730 days (about 2 years) before filing. Recent movers may be required to use the exemptions of their prior state, or fall back to federal exemptions if the prior state is opt-out for non-residents.

Not legal advice. Exemption amounts change. Verify against current Washington statutes before filing. Consult a licensed bankruptcy attorney.
